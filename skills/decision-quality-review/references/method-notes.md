## Method Anchors

From effective decisions:

- Decide whether action is needed.
- Distinguish recurring issues that need a rule from truly exceptional cases.
- Define boundary conditions before choosing.
- Seek disagreement and alternatives.
- Think through the right answer before negotiating acceptable compromise.
- Convert a decision into action, responsibility, and communication.
- Use feedback to compare reality with the assumptions behind the decision.

From consequence asymmetry:

- Decision authority and exposure to failure consequences should not be radically separated.
- Check who receives upside and who bears downside.
- Avoid interventions whose downside can compound or become ruinous.

From the source and user highlights:

- People often filter information to protect existing beliefs.
- Visible success cases can hide the missing evidence of failures.
- People tend to explain others' behavior with inner traits while underweighting context.
- Past investment and ownership can distort current evaluation.
- Group pressure can replace independent judgment.
- The wording and frame of a decision can change what feels reasonable.
- Better decisions require meta-cognition: inspect one's own thinking as part of the situation.

## Skill Boundary

This skill answers:

- What decision is actually being made?
- What frame is shaping the user's options?
- Which biases may be distorting judgment?
- What evidence would change the decision?
- Should the user act, experiment, delay, or reframe?
- What revisit or stop condition should be defined before committing?

This skill does not answer:

- Specialized legal, medical, or financial advice.
- Final ownership of high-stakes decisions.
- Pure argument critique. Use `critical-questioning`.
- Problem decomposition before a decision exists. Use `structured-problem-framing`.

## Common Failure Modes

- Turning the audit into a generic pros-and-cons list.
- Over-diagnosing bias without naming the actual decision.
- Treating the favored option as the only real option.
- Forgetting reversibility and opportunity cost.
- Ignoring disconfirming evidence.
- Letting the agent make the decision instead of preserving user judgment.
